Lens 1
Halving / Cycle
Bitcoin 4-year supply cycle framework
1× weight

Bitcoin operates on a 4-year supply cycle driven by halving events that cut miner rewards in half. Each halving reduces new BTC supply, historically triggering a bull market 12–18 months later followed by a correction. Last halving: April 2024. ATH reached: October 6, 2025 at $126,198.

Identifying broad cycle phase — accumulation, markup, distribution, or markdown. Provides the macro timeline that all other lenses sit within.

Timing precision weak — can be off by months. Institutional ETF ownership is compressing volatility and potentially altering cycle behavior vs prior cycles.

Bullish: confirmed accumulation phase post-correction Neutral: mid-cycle transition Bearish: active distribution phase

Section 3 — Channel Radar Methodology

The Channel Radar is an automated discovery tool that scans the top 100 altcoins for price channel formations and scores them by quality. It surfaces candidates for manual validation in TradingView using Brett Fogle's proprietary indicators. The radar never replaces Brett's indicators — it reduces the manual review workload from ~100 charts to a small ranked shortlist.

Concept
What Is a Price Channel?
Classical technical analysis — parallel trendlines

A price channel forms when an asset's price moves between two parallel trendlines — a support rail (lower boundary) and a resistance rail (upper boundary). The channel captures the rhythm of buying near support and selling near resistance. Channels are among the most reliable classical chart patterns because they represent equilibrium between buyers and sellers within a defined price range.

Both rails slope upward. Higher highs and higher lows. Bullish structure — each pullback to support is a potential entry. The radar's primary target.

Both rails are roughly horizontal. Price consolidating between defined support and resistance. Neutral structure — often precedes a breakout in either direction.

Both rails slope downward. Lower highs and lower lows. Bearish structure — the radar captures these but scores them lower. Watch for a breakout above the upper rail as a potential reversal signal.

Best setup: Price in lower third of ascending channel near support railValidate all candidates in TradingView with Brett's indicators before acting
Algorithm
How the Radar Detects Channels
Pair-based pivot rail detection with geometric scoring

The algorithm scans 365 days of daily OHLC data and identifies pivot highs (local price peaks) and pivot lows (local price troughs) using a lookback window of 3 bars on each side.

Every possible pair of pivot lows is tested as a candidate support rail. A line is drawn through each pair using the actual candle index positions for geometric accuracy. This pair-based approach catches channels where price briefly undercuts a prior low before resuming the trend.

For each candidate rail, the algorithm counts how many pivot lows fall within a 2.5% tolerance band of the rail line. A minimum of 2 touches is required to form a valid rail (3+ preferred for higher confidence). The same process is applied to pivot highs to build the parallel resistance rail.

The vertical distance between rails is calculated using the median offset of all pivot highs above the support rail. Using the median makes the channel height robust against outlier candles.

The percentage of candles that fall within the channel boundaries (with 2.5% tolerance) is measured. Low containment means price frequently breaks through the rails — indicating a weak or false channel.

Each candidate channel receives a composite score from 0–100 based on weighted factors. Only the highest-scoring channel per coin is returned.

Scoring
How Channels Are Scored (0–100)
Weighted composite of geometric quality factors

More confirmed touches of the support rail = stronger channel. 2 touches = line, 3 touches = channel, 4+ touches = high confidence.

Pivot highs respecting the upper rail confirms the channel structure.

% of candles within channel boundaries. Higher containment = cleaner channel structure.

Moderate positive slope (0.05–3%/bar) scores highest. Flat scores moderate. Negative slope loses points. Parabolic (>5%/bar) penalized heavily.

Price in the lower third near support scores maximum. Upper third scores zero.

Longer-running channels are more reliable.

Overextension (price >90% of channel height): -8 pts. Parabolic slope (>5%/bar): -10 pts. Negative slope: -5 pts additional.

200-Day Moving Average (200MA) Lens 5 — Fogle

The average closing price of BTC over the past 200 trading days. The single most widely watched technical indicator by institutional investors globally. Price above 200MA = bull market structure. Price below 200MA = bear market structure. Institutional buy/sell decisions are often triggered at this level.

BullishPrice trading above 200MA and MA sloping upward
BearishPrice trading below 200MA and MA sloping downward
ATH — All-Time High All lenses

The highest price Bitcoin has ever traded. Current ATH is noted in the active brief. ATH represents maximum prior demand. ATH represents maximum prior demand. Breaking above ATH with volume signals new price discovery and historically triggers accelerated appreciation.

Bitcoin Dominance Lens 5 — Fogle

Bitcoin's market cap as a percentage of total crypto market cap. Rising dominance = capital flowing into BTC specifically (BTC strength). Falling dominance = capital rotating into altcoins (altcoin season). For a BTC-focused portfolio, rising dominance is generally positive.

BullishDominance rising — BTC attracting capital vs alts
BearishDominance falling — capital leaving BTC for altcoins
CVDD Floor Model Lens 7 — Woo

Cumulative Value Days Destroyed. Measures the economic value of coin dormancy being spent. The CVDD baseline rises gradually over time reflecting Bitcoin's growing network adoption, creating a rising structural price floor. Has never been violated on a sustained basis in Bitcoin's history.

Safe zonePrice well above CVDD floor — room to absorb selling
WarningPrice approaching CVDD floor — extreme undervaluation / structural support
DXY — US Dollar Index Lens 2 — Macro/Liquidity

Measures the value of the US dollar against a basket of major world currencies (Euro, Yen, Pound, Canadian Dollar, Swedish Krona, Swiss Franc). BTC and DXY historically move inversely. Strong dollar = headwind for BTC. Weak dollar = tailwind for BTC.

Bullish for BTCDXY falling or below 100
Bearish for BTCDXY rising or above 103
ETF Flows Lens 2 & 3

Net capital flowing into or out of US Bitcoin spot ETFs (BlackRock IBIT, Fidelity FBTC, and others). Sustained inflows = institutional accumulation. Sustained outflows = institutional distribution. The most credible real-time institutional demand signal available. See current brief for latest flow data.

BullishNet positive flows sustained over 7–30 days
BearishNet negative flows sustained over 7–30 days

Exchange Netflows Lens 7 — Woo

Net movement of Bitcoin into or out of cryptocurrency exchanges. BTC moved TO exchanges typically signals intent to sell (bearish). BTC moved FROM exchanges (to self-custody wallets) signals long-term holding intent (bullish). Large exchange inflows often precede price drops.

BullishNet outflows — coins leaving exchanges into cold storage
BearishNet inflows — coins moving to exchanges for potential sale
Expanding Triangle Pattern Lens 8 — Brandt

A classical chart pattern where price makes higher highs and lower lows simultaneously, creating a widening price range that looks like a megaphone on a chart. Typically a bearish continuation pattern — widening range indicates increasing volatility and uncertainty, often resolving downward. Brandt has confirmed this pattern on BTC's current chart.

Global M2 Money Supply Lens 2 — Macro/Liquidity

Total money supply (M2 = cash + checking + savings + money market) across all major global economies combined. BTC price has historically followed global M2 with approximately a 12-week lag. Expanding M2 = more capital available for risk assets. Contracting M2 = headwind for BTC.

BullishGlobal M2 expanding year-over-year
BearishGlobal M2 contracting or flat
HODL Waves Lens 7 — Woo

Visualization of Bitcoin supply by age — what percentage of all BTC last moved within different time periods (1 day, 1 week, 1 month, 3 months, 1 year, 2+ years). When older coins stop moving, it signals long-term holder conviction. When younger coin bands expand, it signals new activity — either accumulation or distribution.

BullishLong-term holder bands (>1yr) expanding — conviction holding
BearishShort-term holder bands expanding — distribution or speculation
JpM2 — Japanese M2 Money Supply Lens 6 — Villaverde

Japan's M2 money supply — total yen in circulation plus short-term deposits. Villaverde discovered that JpM2 forward-shifted 12 weeks is one of the strongest leading indicators for BTC price direction. Japan's monetary policy and yen carry trade dynamics have outsized influence on global liquidity.

BullishJpM2 expanding — global liquidity tailwind in ~12 weeks
BearishJpM2 contracting — global liquidity headwind ahead
MVRV Z-Score Lens 7 — Woo

Market Value to Realized Value Z-Score. Compares Bitcoin's current market cap (what the market says BTC is worth) to its Realized Cap (what all holders actually paid), normalized by standard deviation. When market value far exceeds realized value, distribution risk is high. Near or below realized value = historically strong buy zone.

Above 7Extreme overvaluation — historically strong sell zone
3.5 – 7Overvalued — caution, consider reducing exposure
0 – 3.5Fair value — neutral signal
Below 0Undervalued — historically strong accumulation zone
Realized Price Lens 7 — Woo

The average price at which all currently existing Bitcoin last changed hands. Calculated by dividing Realized Cap by total supply. Represents the aggregate cost basis of all Bitcoin holders. Price falling below Realized Price means the average holder is at a loss — historically a strong support zone and accumulation signal.

BullishMarket price well above Realized Price — holders in profit
WarningPrice approaching Realized Price — holders near breakeven, strong support
Extreme buyPrice below Realized Price — average holder at loss, historically strong buy
LTH SOPR — Long-Term Holder Spent Output Profit Ratio Lens 7 — Woo

Measures whether long-term Bitcoin holders (coins held 155+ days) are selling at a profit or a loss. When LTH SOPR drops below 1.0, long-term holders are realizing losses — a historically rare event that signals deep capitulation. Values below 0.7 have historically coincided with major cycle bottoms.

Above 1.3LTHs taking large profits — distribution risk elevated
1.0 – 1.3LTHs in profit, selling normally — neutral
0.7 – 1.0LTHs realizing small losses — mild capitulation
Below 0.7Deep LTH capitulation — historically associated with cycle bottoms

Measured Move / Price Target (Brandt Target) Lens 8 — Brandt

In classical technical analysis, every chart pattern has a mathematically derived price objective called a measured move. The target is calculated by measuring the height of the pattern (from its widest point) and projecting that distance in the direction of the expected breakout. Peter Brandt applies this methodology to BTC's weekly and monthly chart patterns to derive specific price targets. When briefs reference "Brandt target: $XX,XXX" this refers to the measured move objective of whatever classical pattern Brandt has identified on BTC's current chart.

Bearish patternMeasured move projects downward from breakdown point — e.g. expanding triangle targeting $53–55K
Bullish patternMeasured move projects upward from breakout point — e.g. bull flag targeting $250K+
InvalidatedPattern fails when price moves opposite to expected direction with volume — target is cancelled

Note: Brandt targets are time-sensitive analyst calls that change with each new pattern identified. Current target is always in the active brief — this entry explains the methodology only.
